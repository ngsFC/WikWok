# Chapter 8: Histiocytoses

> *Pediatric Oncology: A Comprehensive Guide* — Imbach, Kühne, Arceci

**Author**: Paul Imbach

---

## Overview

Histiocytoses are a heterogeneous group of disorders characterized by **abnormal proliferation of histiocytes** (macrophages, dendritic cells). They range from self-limiting to life-threatening.

### Classification

| Type | Full Name | Cell of Origin |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **LCH** | Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis | Langerhans cell (dendritic) |
| **FEL** | Familial Erythrophagocytic Lymphohistiocytosis | Macrophage (T-cell activation) |
| **IAHS** | Infection-Associated Hemophagocytic Syndrome | Reactive macrophage |
| **MH** | Malignant Histiocytosis | Malignant macrophage/monocyte |

---

## Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis (LCH)

### Definition

Clonal proliferation of **Langerhans cells** (CD1a+, S100+, Birbeck granules on EM). Previously known as histiocytosis X (Letterer-Siwe, Hand-Schüller-Christian, eosinophilic granuloma).

### Incidence

- 3–5 cases per million children per year
- Peak: 1–3 years for multisystem; any age for single system
- Boys > girls (3:2)

### Clinical Presentation

| Form | Age | Sites | Behavior |
|------|-----|-------|---------|
| **Unifocal** (eosinophilic granuloma) | Older children/adults | Bone (skull, femur, vertebra) | Self-limiting |
| **Multifocal unisystem** (Hand-Schüller-Christian) | 2–6 years | Bone, skin, DI triad | Chronic progressive |
| **Multifocal multisystem** (Letterer-Siwe) | <2 years | Skin, bone marrow, liver, spleen, lung | Life-threatening |

**Classic Hand-Schüller-Christian triad**: lytic skull lesions + exophthalmos + diabetes insipidus (only 10% complete)

**Skin**: seborrhea-like eruptions, petechiae, nodules — often misdiagnosed as eczema

**Bone**: "punched-out" lytic lesions — skull most common; vertebra plana (flattened vertebrae)

**Lung**: interstitial infiltrates, honeycombing → spontaneous pneumothorax

**Endocrine**: diabetes insipidus (hypothalamic infiltration) — most common sequela

### Diagnosis

- Biopsy: **CD1a+, S100+** by immunohistochemistry; Birbeck granules (tennis racket-shaped) on EM
- Skeletal survey (not bone scan — LCH is cold)
- Chest X-ray, PFTs if lung involved
- Urine osmolality for DI

### Risk Stratification

| Risk Group | Features |
|-----------|---------|
| **Low risk** | Single system (bone, skin, LN) |
| **High risk** | Multisystem + organ dysfunction (liver, spleen, bone marrow, lung) |

### Treatment

| Setting | Treatment |
|---------|-----------|
| Unifocal bone | Curettage ± local steroid injection; low-dose RT (6–8 Gy) for inaccessible sites |
| DI | Vasopressin (DDAVP) |
| Multisystem low-risk | Vinblastine + prednisone × 6–12 months |
| Multisystem high-risk | Vinblastine + prednisone; etoposide; consider SCT for refractory |

### Prognosis

- Unifocal: excellent; ~20% spontaneous regression
- Multisystem without organ dysfunction: **>80% survival**
- Multisystem with organ dysfunction: **<50% survival**
- Sequelae: DI (20–40%), growth failure, pulmonary fibrosis, neurodegenerative LCH

## Familial Erythrophagocytic Lymphohistiocytosis (FEL)

### Definition

Autosomal recessive disorder with **uncontrolled macrophage activation** and erythrophagocytosis, due to defective cytotoxic T-lymphocyte and NK cell function.

**Genetic basis**: Mutations in perforin (*PRF1*), MUNC13-4, syntaxin 11, STXBP2 — impaired granule-mediated cytotoxicity.

### Incidence

- 1 per 50,000–100,000 births
- Presents in first 2 years; often within weeks of birth
- Familial — consanguinity common

### Clinical Presentation

- **Fever**, hepatosplenomegaly, jaundice
- Cytopenias (anemia, thrombocytopenia, neutropenia)
- Neurological involvement (irritability, seizures, meningismus)
- Hyperferritinemia, hypertriglyceridemia, hypofibrinogenemia
- CSF pleocytosis, hemophagocytosis in CSF

### Diagnostic Criteria (HLH-2004 — ≥5 of 8)

1. Fever
2. Splenomegaly
3. Cytopenias ≥2 cell lines
4. Hypertriglyceridemia and/or hypofibrinogenemia
5. Hemophagocytosis in BM/spleen/LN
6. Low/absent NK cell activity
7. Ferritin ≥500 µg/L
8. Elevated sIL-2R (soluble CD25)

### Treatment

**HLH-94/HLH-2004 protocol:**
- Induction: dexamethasone + etoposide (8 weeks)
- CNS involvement: intrathecal methotrexate
- Continuation: cyclosporine A + dexamethasone pulses
- **Curative**: allogeneic SCT (only cure)

**Prognosis**: Fatal without SCT; 50–60% long-term survival after SCT.

## Infection-Associated Hemophagocytic Syndrome (IAHS)

### Definition

**Reactive (secondary) hemophagocytic syndrome** triggered by infection — not inherited. Massive cytokine storm (IFN-γ, IL-6, TNF) causes uncontrolled macrophage activation.

### Triggers

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **Viral** | EBV (most common), CMV, HSV, HHV-6, HIV, parvovirus B19 |
| **Bacterial** | *Mycobacterium*, *Salmonella*, gram-negative sepsis |
| **Fungal** | *Candida*, *Aspergillus* (in immunocompromised) |
| **Parasitic** | *Leishmania*, malaria |

### Clinical Features

Similar to FEL: fever, hepatosplenomegaly, cytopenias, hemophagocytosis on BM biopsy.

**Key distinction from FEL**: reactive/secondary → treat underlying infection first; no family history; older onset.

### Treatment

1. Treat underlying infection aggressively
2. If severe: corticosteroids ± IVIG
3. If life-threatening or EBV-driven: etoposide + cyclosporine (HLH protocol)
4. SCT generally not needed (unless underlying immunodeficiency)

**Prognosis**: Good if infection controlled. Mortality ~10–30% in severe forms.

## Malignant Histiocytosis (MH)

### Definition

True malignant neoplasm of **macrophage/monocyte lineage** — rare, distinct from LCH. Must be distinguished from ALCL and other lymphomas.

### Features

- Aggressive systemic disease: fever, weight loss, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy
- Cytopenias from marrow infiltration
- Hemophagocytosis on histology
- Immunophenotype: CD68+, lysozyme+, CD1a−

### Treatment

- AML-type or lymphoma-type chemotherapy
- SCT for eligible patients
- Poor prognosis overall
