# Chapter 10: Neuroblastoma

> *Pediatric Oncology: A Comprehensive Guide* — Imbach, Kühne, Arceci

**Author**: Paul Imbach

---

## Definition

- Malignant embryonal tumor of **precursor cells of sympathetic ganglia and adrenal medulla**
- Entity characterized by:
  - Occasional **spontaneous regression** and differentiation to benign tumor (especially in infants <12 months)
  - Usually extremely malignant course in children in advanced stage

---

## Incidence

- **8% of all neoplasia** in childhood
- ~11 per million children <16 years annually
- **Most frequent malignant neoplasia in infants**
- Mean age at diagnosis: **2.5 years**

### Cumulative Age Distribution

| Age | Cumulative % |
|-----|-------------|
| <1 year | 35% |
| <2 years | 50% |
| <4 years | 75% |
| <10 years | 90% |

- Rarely observed in adolescents and adults
- Boy:girl ratio = 1.1:1.0

---

## Etiology and Pathogenesis

- Etiology unknown
- Neuroblastic precursor cells in autopsies of infants <3 months who died of other causes: 40× higher than expected
- Associations: neurofibromatosis, Hirschsprung disease, heterochromia iridis
- Familial occurrence: sibling and twin disease rarely described
- Tumor cell chromosomal changes detectable in most patients

---

## Molecular Cytogenetics

Key prognostic factors:

| Age at Diagnosis | *MYCN* | DNA | *TRK1* | Stage | Prognosis |
|-----------------|--------|-----|--------|-------|-----------|
| <12 months | Usually normal | Hyperdiploid | High | 1, 2, 4S | **95%** |
| >12 months | Usually normal | Diploid | Low | 3, 4 | **50%** |
| 1–5 years | **Commonly amplified** | Diploid | Low | 3, 4 | **25%** |

**Key molecular factors:**
- ***MYCN* amplification**: worst prognostic factor
- **DNA index**: hyperdiploid → favorable; diploid → unfavorable
- ***TRK1*** (neurotrophin receptor): high expression → favorable
- **Chromosome 1p deletion**: tumor suppressor loss → unfavorable
- **Chromosome 14 deletion**: unfavorable

---

## Pathology

### Macroscopic Features
Pale gray, soft tumors with necrosis and calcification; large tumors have unclear demarcations and are highly invasive.

### Microscopic Features
- High variability from undifferentiated neuroblastoma to ganglioneuroblastoma to differentiated ganglioneuroma
- **Small round-cell sarcoma** histologically — must differentiate from:
  - PNET (primitive neuroectodermal tumor)
  - Embryonal undifferentiated rhabdomyosarcoma
  - Retinoblastoma
  - Ewing sarcoma
  - Lymphoma
- Well-differentiated form: polymorphic nuclei separated by fibrillar material, rosette arrangements
- **Electron microscopy**: neurofilaments, neural tubules, neurosecretory granules
- **IHC**: immunoperoxidase, neuron-specific enolase (NSE), fluorescence for catecholamines

---

## Clinical Manifestations

Occurrence in any area with **sympathetic nervous tissue**.

### Primary Locations

| Location | Frequency |
|----------|----------|
| **Abdomen** | 65% |
| Adrenal medulla / sympathetic ganglia | 46% |
| Posterior mediastinum | 15% |
| Pelvic | 4% |
| Head and neck | 3% |
| Others | 8% |

### Common Symptoms
Weight loss, fever, abdominal disturbances, irritability, bone/joint pain, child won't stand/walk, pallor, lassitude.

### Symptoms of Catecholamine Production
Paroxysmal flushing/sweating/pallor, headache, **hypertension**, palpitations.

### Paraneoplastic Syndromes
- **VIP syndrome**: untreatable diarrhea + hypokalemia (VIP secretion) — 5–10%
- **Opsoclonus-myoclonus** ("dancing eyes-dancing feet"): mostly in well-differentiated neuroblastoma — paradoxically favorable prognosis
- Anemia (bone marrow infiltration or intratumoral hemorrhage)

### Local Symptoms and Classic Signs

| Region | Signs |
|--------|-------|
| **Eyes** | Periorbital edema, yellow-brown ecchymoses ("raccoon eyes"), exophthalmos, opsoclonus |
| **Neck** | Cervical lymphadenopathy, **Horner syndrome** (enophthalmos, miosis, ptosis, anhydrosis) |
| **Chest/Vertebrae** | Cough/dyspnea (tracheal compression), **dumbbell tumor** (foraminal extension), neurological deficits |
| **Abdomen** | Retroperitoneal firm mass crossing midline; liver "pepper type" hepatomegaly in infants |
| **Skin** | Blue subcutaneous nodules turning white after palpation (catecholamine release) |
| **Bone** | Bone pain, lytic lesions in skull/long bones, periosteal reactions |
| **Bone marrow** | Infiltration >50% of patients; thrombocytopenia + anemia → advanced stage |

---

## Metastatic Spread

- Lymphatic and/or hematogenous spread
- Present at diagnosis in: 40–50% <1 year; **70% >1 year**
- Metastatic sites: bone marrow, bone, liver, skin; rarely brain/spinal cord/heart/lung
- Lymph node involvement in 35% with local neuroblastoma

## Laboratory Findings

### Urinary Catecholamine Metabolites (Tyrosine Metabolism)

| Metabolite | Sensitivity |
|-----------|-------------|
| Vanillylmandelic acid (VMA) | 95% |
| Homovanillic acid (HVA) | 90% |
| 3-methoxy-4-hydroxyphenylglycol (MHPG) | 97% |

> Urinary catecholamine metabolites are excellent **tumor markers for follow-up**.

### Bone Marrow
- Aspiration + biopsy at ≥2 locations required

## Diagnostic Imaging

| Modality | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| **MIBG scintigraphy** (¹³¹I-MIBG) | Radiolabeled; specific and sensitive for primary + metastatic disease |
| **CT/MRI** | Tumor size, extension, metastases (abdominal, hepatic, skeletal, mediastinal, CNS) |
| Conventional X-ray | Calcifications in tumor (abdominal); skeletal survey for cortical bone metastases |
| Myelography | If neural compression/paraparesis present |

## Differential Diagnosis

- Osteomyelitis, rheumatoid arthritis
- VIP syndrome: infectious/autoimmune intestinal disorders
- Opsoclonus/ataxia: neurological disorders
- Infants with hepatomegaly: storage diseases
- Other small round-cell tumors (see pathology above)

---

## Staging (International Neuroblastoma Staging System — INSS)

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **I** | Localized tumor, complete gross excision; ipsilateral LN negative |
| **IIA** | Localized tumor, incomplete excision; ipsilateral nonadherent LN negative |
| **IIB** | Localized tumor ± complete excision; ipsilateral nonadherent LN positive; contralateral LN negative |
| **III** | Unresectable crossing midline; or unilateral with contralateral LN; or midline with bilateral LN |
| **IV** | Dissemination to distant LN, bone, bone marrow, liver, skin, or other organs (except 4S) |
| **IVS** | Localized primary (I/IIA/IIB) + dissemination limited to skin, liver, bone marrow — **infants <1 year** |

## Therapy

Treatment depends on **age, stage, localization, and molecular features** at diagnosis.

### Surgical Procedure
- Initial surgery: staging + biopsy ± tumor excision without injury to vital structures
- Radical resection often possible after chemo/RT
- Complications: hemorrhage, Horner syndrome, nephrectomy (if adherent to kidney)

### Chemotherapy
- Cyclophosphamide/ifosfamide + cisplatin + doxorubicin + etoposide (epipodophyllotoxin)
- Induction phase → eventual residual tumor resection → maintenance chemo ± RT

### Radiotherapy
- Neuroblastoma is radiosensitive
- Indications: shrinking large masses, decompressing intraspinal tumors, palliative
- Limited by: patient age, long-term sequelae, combination with chemo

### Risk-Adapted Management

**Low risk** (Stages I, IIA, IIB, IVS — DNA index >1, no *MYCN* amplification, favorable histology):
- Radical resection; observe
- Stage IVS <12 months without *MYCN* amplification: **85–92% cure rate** with staging + eventual resection; low-dose liver RT (1.5–6 Gy) if rapid hepatomegaly
- Chemotherapy/RT only if needed

**Intermediate and High-Risk** (Stage II with *MYCN* amp; Stages III, IV, IVS with *MYCN* amp or unfavorable histology):
- Induction chemo → surgery → consolidation → maintenance ± RT
- Persistent disease: **high-dose chemo + autologous SCT**
- Minimal residual disease (MRD): retinoids for differentiation; MIBG radiolabeled therapy
- Monoclonal antibody against neuroblastoma cell antigen (anti-GD2: 3F8, GD2a)
- Allogeneic SCT for graft-versus-tumor effect (still experimental)

### Therapy in Relapse
- Topotecan, paclitaxel (Taxol), irinotecan, etoposide (palliative or curative goal)
- Radiolabeled MIBG therapy

---

## Prognosis

- Depends on **age** (<18 months favorable), **stage**, **localization**, and molecular features
- Thoracic, presacral, cervical primaries: favorable
- Lymph node involvement: poor prognosis

| Risk Group | Prognosis |
|-----------|----------|
| **Low risk** | >90% long-term survival |
| **Intermediate risk** | 60–78% response; 40–60% EFS at 3 years after consolidation |
| **High risk** | 40–60% EFS after double high-dose chemo + SCT |

## Special Forms

### Ganglioneuroblastoma
- Older children/adolescents; adrenal medulla + posterior mediastinum
- Often large; histological features of both neuroblastoma and ganglioneuroma
- Management: as for neuroblastoma

### Ganglioneuroma
- **Benign tumor**; adolescents and young adults
- Incidental diagnosis from thoracic X-ray
- Urinary catecholamines within normal range; encapsulated
- Histology: ganglia cells, Nissl granules, neurofibrils, myxomatous stroma
- Therapy: resection; Horner syndrome possible post-surgery (mediastinal location)

### Olfactory Neuroblastoma (Esthesioneuroblastoma)
- Older children and adults; peaks at 11–20 and 50–60 years
- Symptoms: nasal obstruction, epistaxis, anosmia, rhinorrhea, pain
- Metastatic spread to LN, lung, pleura, bone in ~25%; brain in 14%
- Therapy: radical resection + RT; ~2/3 cured

### Neuroblastoma from Organ of Zuckerkandl
- Midline tumor; management same as neuroblastoma

### Pheochromocytoma
- Origin: **chromaffin cells of the neural crest**
- Mostly adrenal gland; 20% bilateral; occasionally multifocal
- Occurs in children >10 years
- Symptoms: paroxysmal flushing, pallor, sweating, headache, palpitations, **hypertension**, weight loss, polydipsia
- Urinary catecholamines markedly elevated
- Diagnostics: US, MRI, scintigraphy
- Therapy: **preoperative α- and β-blockade** (prevent hypertensive crisis) + surgical resection; intensive care surveillance
