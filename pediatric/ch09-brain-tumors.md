# Chapter 9: Brain Tumors

> *Pediatric Oncology: A Comprehensive Guide* — Imbach, Kühne, Arceci

**Authors**: Stefan Rutkowski, Hansjörg Bode, Andreas Gnekow

## Epidemiology

- **Most common solid tumor** in children; 2nd most common malignancy overall
- **19% of all childhood cancers** (after leukemia)
- Incidence: ~25–30 per million children/year
- Peak: 5–10 years
- Slight male predominance

### Frequency by Tumor Type

| Tumor | Frequency (%) |
|-------|--------------|
| **Astrocytoma** (all grades) | ~40 |
| **Brain stem tumors** | 10–25 |
| **Medulloblastoma/PNET** | 15–20 |
| **Ependymoma** | 6–9 |
| **Craniopharyngioma** | ~7 |
| Germ cell tumors | 3–4 |
| Choroid plexus tumors | 2–3 |
| Other | remainder |

## Clinical Manifestations

### Symptoms by Location

| Location | Symptoms |
|----------|---------|
| **Supratentorial** (cortex) | Seizures, hemiparesis, visual field defects, aphasia |
| **Infratentorial** (posterior fossa) | Ataxia, nystagmus, headache (morning), vomiting, cranial nerve palsy |
| **Hypothalamus/optic chiasm** | Visual loss, diencephalic syndrome (emaciation), DI, precocious puberty |
| **Brain stem** | Cranial nerve palsies, long tract signs (hemi-/quadriplegia), ataxia — NO hydrocephalus initially |
| **Spinal cord** | Back pain, scoliosis, weakness, bladder/bowel dysfunction |

### Signs of Increased ICP

- **Headache** (worse in morning, with Valsalva)
- **Vomiting** (projectile, not preceded by nausea)
- **Papilledema** (late sign)
- In infants: bulging fontanelle, macrocephaly, "sunset sign"
- **Cushing triad** (hypertension + bradycardia + irregular breathing) — late, severe

## Diagnosis

### Neuroimaging

| Modality | Use |
|----------|-----|
| **MRI with Gd** | Gold standard — best soft tissue; preferred pre-/post-op |
| CT | Acute/emergency; detects calcification, hemorrhage, bony erosion |
| **PET** | Metabolic activity; distinguishes recurrence from radiation necrosis |
| MR spectroscopy | NAA ↓, choline ↑ in tumors |
| Spinal MRI | "Drop metastases" — required for medulloblastoma, ependymoma, PNET |

### Tumor Markers (CSF and serum)

| Marker | Tumor |
|--------|-------|
| **AFP** | Non-germinomatous germ cell tumors (yolk sac) |
| **β-HCG** | Choriocarcinoma, mixed germ cell |
| **LDH** | Lymphoma, high-grade tumors |
| PLAP | Germinoma |

## General Therapeutic Principles

### Surgery

- **Maximal safe resection** is the cornerstone — extent of resection is a major prognostic factor
- Goals: tissue diagnosis, debulking, CSF circulation, symptom relief
- Neuronavigation, intraoperative MRI, awake craniotomy (older children) for eloquent areas
- Complete resection often curative for low-grade astrocytoma

### Radiotherapy

- **Standard RT**: 54–60 Gy focal (high-grade); 23.4–36 Gy craniospinal (CSI) for disseminated tumors
- Proton beam therapy: reduces dose to normal tissue — preferred in young children
- **Avoid RT <3 years** (neurotoxicity, white matter damage, hormonal sequelae)
- Side effects: leukoencephalopathy, growth hormone deficiency, neurocognitive decline

### Chemotherapy

- Increasingly used to **delay or reduce RT** in young children
- Active agents: carboplatin, vincristine, temozolomide, etoposide, cyclophosphamide, cisplatin
- High-dose chemo + autologous SCT for some young children with malignant tumors

## Astrocytic Tumors

### Low-Grade Astrocytoma (LGA) — WHO Grade I–II

**Most common pediatric brain tumor** — especially cerebellar pilocytic astrocytoma (grade I).

| Subtype | Location | Features |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Pilocytic astrocytoma** (grade I) | Cerebellum, optic pathway, hypothalamus | Biphasic pattern; Rosenthal fibers; KIAA1549-BRAF fusion |
| Fibrillary astrocytoma (grade II) | Cerebral hemispheres | Infiltrating; no clear border |
| Diffuse intrinsic pontine glioma (grade II) | Pons | See "brain stem" section |

**Treatment LGA:**
- Complete resection → **observation** (often curative for pilocytic)
- Residual/progressive: carboplatin + vincristine (first-line in young children)
- RT: for progression after chemo; avoid <8 years if possible
- **Response rate: 65–75%**; 10-year OS ~90% for pilocytic

### High-Grade Astrocytoma (HGA) — WHO Grade III–IV

| Grade | Type | Median Survival |
|-------|------|----------------|
| III | Anaplastic astrocytoma | 24–36 months |
| IV | **Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM)** | 12–15 months |

**Features**: necrosis, endothelial proliferation (GBM); rapid progression; poor prognosis.

**Treatment HGA:**
- Surgery (maximal resection) + RT (59.4 Gy) + temozolomide
- Adjuvant temozolomide (Stupp protocol — adult data applied to pediatrics)
- Clinical trials: VEGF inhibitors, targeted therapy (IDH1/2, H3K27M mutations in pediatric GBM)
- **2-year OS: ~20–30% for GBM**

## Optic Pathway/Hypothalamic Glioma

- **3–5% of pediatric brain tumors**
- **NF1 associated in 35–50%**; bilateral optic nerve gliomas are pathognomonic for NF1
- Histology: usually pilocytic astrocytoma (grade I)
- Symptoms: visual loss (acuity ↓, visual field defects), nystagmus, diencephalic syndrome (emaciation in infants)

**Treatment:**
- NF1 + stable vision: **observation** (many do not progress)
- Progressive/symptomatic: carboplatin + vincristine chemotherapy
- Surgery: biopsy only (resection risks blindness)
- RT: last resort (risk of moyamoya, hormone deficiency, cognitive decline)
- **Prognosis**: 10-year OS ~85–90%; visual outcome highly variable

## Brain Stem Tumors

- **10–25% of pediatric brain tumors**
- Pons most affected; also midbrain and medulla

### Classification

| Type | Location | Features | Prognosis |
|------|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Diffuse intrinsic pontine glioma (DIPG)** | Pons (diffuse) | H3K27M mutation; cannot biopsy safely | **Median OS 9–12 months** |
| Focal brain stem glioma | Midbrain/medulla, dorsal exophytic | Well circumscribed; often pilocytic | Good (OS ~80%) |
| Tectal glioma | Tectal plate | Obstructive hydrocephalus; slow growth | Excellent |

**DIPG features:**
- Peak 5–9 years; 50–90% of brain stem tumors
- Triad: cranial nerve palsies + long tract signs + ataxia; no hydrocephalus early
- MRI: T2 hyperintense, >2/3 of pons, no contrast enhancement
- **No resection possible**; biopsy now performed for molecular profiling
- H3.3 K27M mutation → epigenetic dysregulation
- Treatment: RT 54 Gy focal (palliative); ONC201 (H3K27M targeted) — investigational
- No standard effective chemotherapy

## Medulloblastoma

**Most common malignant pediatric brain tumor** (15–20% of all pediatric brain tumors).

### Biology and Molecular Groups (WHO 2016+)

| Group | Features | Prognosis |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| **WNT** | CTNNB1 mutation; adults > children | Excellent (>90% OS) |
| **SHH** | PTCH1/SMO/SUFU; desmoplastic/nodular histology | Intermediate |
| **Group 3** | MYC amplification; infants/children | Poor |
| **Group 4** | Most common; isochromosome 17q | Intermediate |

### Clinical Features

- Posterior fossa; obstructive hydrocephalus (→ headache, vomiting)
- "Drop metastases" via CSF: spinal cord involvement in 20–30%
- Classic histology: densely packed small blue cells; Homer Wright rosettes

### Risk Stratification

| Risk | Criteria |
|------|---------|
| **Standard risk** | Age >3; M0; residual <1.5 cm² |
| **High risk** | Age <3; M1–M4; residual ≥1.5 cm²; large-cell/anaplastic |

### Treatment

- **Surgery**: maximal safe resection
- **Craniospinal irradiation (CSI)**: 23.4 Gy (standard risk) or 36 Gy (high risk) + posterior fossa boost to 54–55.8 Gy
- **Chemotherapy**: cisplatin + carboplatin + vincristine + cyclophosphamide + etoposide
- Young children (<3 years): chemo-first strategy to delay/avoid RT

### Prognosis

| Risk Group | 5-Year OS |
|-----------|----------|
| Standard risk | **70–85%** |
| High risk | **50–60%** |
| WNT subgroup | **>90%** |

**Sequelae**: neurocognitive decline (IQ ↓20–30 points after CSI), GH deficiency, hearing loss (cisplatin), cerebellar mutism post-surgery.

## Ependymoma

- **6–9% of pediatric brain tumors**
- Origin: ependymal cells lining ventricles and central canal
- Most common location: **posterior fossa (60%)** → 4th ventricle
- Spinal ependymoma in older children/adults
- CSF dissemination: **"drop metastases"** in 7–12%

### Molecular Groups (WHO 2021)

| Group | Location | Molecular | Prognosis |
|-------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| ST-RELA | Supratentorial | RELA fusion | Intermediate-poor |
| ST-YAP1 | Supratentorial | YAP1 fusion | Favorable |
| PF-A | Posterior fossa, infants | Methylation group A | Poor |
| PF-B | Posterior fossa, older | Methylation group B | Favorable |
| SP | Spinal | NF2 mutation | Favorable |

### Treatment

- **Total resection** + focal RT (54–59.4 Gy) → **66–75% OS after radical resection**
- Second-look surgery if incomplete resection
- Chemotherapy: limited efficacy; used in young children to delay RT
- Spinal staging MRI mandatory

## Craniopharyngioma

- **~7% of pediatric brain tumors**; slow-growing, benign (WHO grade I)
- Origin: Rathke's pouch remnants (suprasellar/sellar)
- Histology: adamantinomatous (children) vs papillary (adults)
- CTNNB1 (β-catenin) mutation in adamantinomatous; BRAF V600E in papillary

### Clinical Features

- **Headache + visual disturbance** (optic chiasm compression)
- **Endocrine**: GH deficiency (>70%), hypothyroidism, DI, obesity, precocious or delayed puberty
- Calcification on CT (90% in children) — "eggshell" calcification
- T1 bright cystic content ("machine oil")

### Treatment

- Surgery: **limited resection** (protect hypothalamus) + RT — reduces sequelae vs aggressive resection
- Intracavitary treatment: bleomycin or IFN-α for cystic tumors
- RT: fractionated focal RT or proton beam
- **Prognosis**: excellent for tumor control (>90% 10-year OS); but major morbidity:
  - Hypothalamic obesity (>50%)
  - Panhypopituitarism requiring lifelong hormone replacement
  - Visual impairment, neurocognitive deficits

## Pineal Tumors and Intracranial Germ Cell Tumors

- Located in pineal region and/or suprasellar
- More common in males; Asian > Caucasian

### Classification

| Type | Markers | Radiosensitivity | EFS |
|------|---------|-----------------|-----|
| **Germinoma** | β-HCG mildly ↑, AFP normal | Highly radiosensitive | **>90%** |
| Mature teratoma | Normal | Surgery alone | Excellent |
| Non-germinomatous (NGGCT) | AFP ↑ and/or β-HCG ↑ | Moderate | ~60–70% |
| Choriocarcinoma | β-HCG very high | Poor | Poor |

**Diagnosis**: MRI + serum and CSF AFP + β-HCG (biopsy if markers negative)

**Treatment:**
- Germinoma: CSI (24 Gy) + boost or chemo + reduced RT → >90% EFS
- NGGCT: chemo (platinum-based BEP) + CSI + surgery
- Hydrocephalus: endoscopic third ventriculostomy (preferred over shunt)

---

## Meningioma

- Rare in children (<2%)
- Associated with NF2 (bilateral acoustic neuromas + spinal meningiomas)
- Treatment: surgical resection; RT for recurrence/unresectable

---

## Intramedullary Spinal Cord Tumors

- **5–10% of CNS tumors in children**
- Types: astrocytoma (70%), ependymoma (30%)
- Symptoms: back pain, scoliosis, progressive weakness, sphincter dysfunction
- MRI essential for diagnosis and surgical planning
- Treatment: surgery (goal = maximum safe resection); RT for high-grade
- **5-year OS: ~60–80%** for low-grade

---

## Late Effects and Follow-Up

| Domain | Common Sequelae |
|--------|----------------|
| **Endocrine** | GH deficiency, hypothyroidism, DI, gonadal failure, adrenal insufficiency |
| **Neurocognitive** | Memory, attention, processing speed — especially after RT and young age |
| **Hearing** | Cisplatin-induced sensorineural hearing loss |
| **Vision** | Optic pathway involvement; RT-related vasculopathy |
| **Neurological** | Motor/sensory deficits, cerebellar syndrome |
| **Secondary tumors** | Radiation-induced meningioma, glioma (10–15 years later) |

**Follow-up protocol:**
- MRI q3 months (year 1), q6 months (year 2–3), annually thereafter
- Annual endocrine evaluation; audiology; neuropsychological testing; ophthalmology
